05 · Gaps & Fixes

WHAT'S MISSING
& HOW TO FIX IT

Honest assessment. These aren't dealbreakers — but they're things Nike recruiters will notice. Here's how to close them fast.

Gap

No Nike-Adjacent Brand on Resume

You've never worked for a sportswear, footwear, or athletic brand. That's the most common recruiter hesitation at Nike.

Fix

Build a Spec Project Now

One strong, Nike-spec self-initiated case study in your portfolio erases this concern. It shows initiative and proves you understand the category without needing to be told. Do this before you apply.

Gap

CMF Work Isn't Visually Documented

You talk about CMF in your resume, but if you can't show material/finish decision-making visually in your portfolio, it reads as buzzword rather than skill.

Fix

Document the Candle Line as a CMF Case Study

Photograph or recreate the material boards, finish callouts, and design decisions from the candle product line. Even a reconstructed process board is better than nothing. This is your CMF proof point.

Gap

Freelance 2020–Present Reads as Unfocused

Political campaigns + candles + grocery branding + private label = scattered. Recruiters scan for cohesion, and a scattered freelance period raises flags at a brand-focused company like Nike.

Fix

Re-frame as "Studio Practice"

Lead with the 2–3 projects most relevant to the specific Nike role you're applying to. In your portfolio, only show work that maps to that role. Your resume already positions it as "Freelance & Studio Projects" — lean into the studio framing and curate ruthlessly.

Gap

No Footwear-Specific Work

Nike is fundamentally a footwear company. Footwear color blocking and upper graphic design are specific skills they test for. Your resume mentions it in skills but there's no proof.

Fix

Add a Footwear Color Study to Your Spec Project

Within your Nike spec case study, include one footwear color blocking exploration — even as a supporting slide. Use an existing Nike silhouette. Show you understand upper/midsole/outsole color relationships and material callouts.

07 · Nike Outreach

MESSAGES THAT
DON'T FEEL DESPERATE

Two versions. Use whichever fits the person. The rule: specific over generic, curious over needy, short over long.

Version A — For a Nike Designer or Design Manager (Cold)

"Hi [Name] — I've been following your work on [specific project/team/category] and wanted to reach out directly rather than just applying into the void.

I'm a Senior Designer with a background in color, graphics, and product — most recently building seasonal systems for retail at scale and leading CMF product development independently. I'm exploring opportunities at Nike specifically because I want to work where design decisions connect directly to product outcomes, not just brand assets.

If you're open to a 15-minute conversation about your team or the design culture on [specific team/category], I'd genuinely value it. Happy to share my portfolio first if that's easier."

Version B — For a Nike Recruiter or Talent Partner

"Hi [Name] — I'm a Portland-based Senior Designer with 13 years across color, graphics, and product design — most recently driving $19.83M in product sales through seasonal design systems and leading full CMF product creation for national retail.

I'm actively targeting Senior Graphic Designer or Senior Color Designer roles at Nike and wanted to connect directly. I have a strong portfolio showing concept-to-shelf work and I'm happy to share it with you or the relevant team.

Is there a right moment to connect, or a better person on your team to speak with about open roles in [Apparel / Footwear / Jordan / Sportswear]?"

Rules for Outreach

Always reference something specific

A campaign, a product launch, a team. Generic admiration reads as lazy. Specificity shows you actually pay attention to Nike's work.

Never ask for a job in the first message

Ask for a conversation, input, or perspective. The relationship comes first. The referral or interview follows naturally if you're a real fit.

Follow up once, not three times

Seven days after the first message, one follow-up. Something like: "Still happy to share my portfolio if timing works." Then let it go.

Your portfolio link does the heavy lifting

Every outreach message should have your portfolio link. If the work is strong, it closes the loop without you having to say anything else.
